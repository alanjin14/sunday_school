## Sermon Summary (3 Main Points)

### 1. We Are Reconciled to God for All Time (vv. 9-11)

- Because of the fall, we begin in rebellion against God — our human nature is inherently rebellious to authority.
- There is a fundamental allure of control: we want to call all the shots in our destiny.
- Satan sows seeds of doubt: "Did God really say...?" and the fruit of that doubt is trusting our own judgment over God's.
- We are practically telling God "thanks but no thanks" — refusing to reciprocate His love and giving our love to something else.
- Yet God still desired to pursue us — that's a crazy kind of love.
- Reconciliation is totally one-sided: there is nothing we could do except get ourselves in the posture of receiving all grace from God.
- Two things happen through reconciliation:
  - The barriers to approaching God are removed — sin is abolished and can no longer keep us from enjoying God's presence.
  - We survive encountering God at all — unlike Moses who had to be shielded from God's full glory, and the high priest who entered the Most Holy Place only once a year with a rope tied around him.
- Our reconciliation to God is tied to the permanent and perfectly sufficient sacrifice of Jesus.

### 2. We Witness the Depth of God's Love Through Christ (vv. 6-8)

- Verse 6: Christ died for the ungodly.
- Verse 7: Someone might die for a righteous or good person.
- Verse 8: We were not even righteous, let alone good — but Christ died for us. This is the gospel.
- Knowing we would continue to sin today, Christ died for us.
- Do we live as if there was someone who loved us enough to die for us?
- Many movies depict the hero's sacrifice (Star Wars, Saving Private Ryan, The Lion King, Big Hero 6, Avengers: Endgame) — the surviving characters change their ways because of the hero's love.
- Are we participating in this world any differently than if we did not know Jesus?
- This kind of discipleship sees the tempting things of the world and says no — because the fulfillment from spending time with God is so much sweeter.

### 3. We Handle True Suffering in Life (vv. 1-5)

- Hope is developed from proven character → which comes from perseverance → which comes from tribulations/suffering.
- There's a difference between embracing discomfort with faith in a reconciled relationship vs. simply facing hardship.
- Sanctification is not always smooth — like getting stronger requires tearing muscle fibers, faith must be stretched.
- We may have a "phobia of hardship" — turning our pursuit of human flourishing into one less prepared for discomfort.
- Microscopic vs. telescopic perspective: we magnify small problems (microscopic) when we should magnify God's love and grace (telescopic).
- Once we regularly embrace how big God's love is, perseverance, character, and hope become fruit of a trusting, reconciled relationship.
- Example of Abraham (Genesis 22): asked to sacrifice Isaac, Abraham trusted God could keep all His promises — and God provided a suitable sacrifice, one-sidedly securing reconciliation while stretching Abraham's faith.

---

## Key Takeaway

> "When we can witness the depth of God's love, we can handle true suffering in life — because we are reconciled to God for all time."

---

## Application

- **For salvation:** We cannot do anything to earn it. We cannot achieve it by our effort. There is nothing we can do except accept it.
- **For daily life:** Are we living as if someone loved us enough to die for us? Does the sacrificial love of Jesus inform what we allow into our eyes and what takes up room in our hearts?
- **For suffering:** Take a telescopic view of God's love rather than a microscopic view of our problems. Embrace that sanctification involves being stretched through difficulty.
- **For our community:** Everyone out there — still broken, rebellious, unrighteous — is someone God desires to reconcile. We were all like this too.
